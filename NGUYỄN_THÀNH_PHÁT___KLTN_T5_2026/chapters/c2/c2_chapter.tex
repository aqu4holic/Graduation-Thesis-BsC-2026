\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{chap:background}

This chapter presents the theoretical foundations directly relevant to the problem and solution developed in the thesis: (1) causal graph theory and the definition of the eight variable roles, (2) statistical features used to represent observational data as edge tensors, (3) core deep learning architectural components (residual Conv1D, self-attention), and (4) an overview of existing approaches on the ADIA Lab Causal Discovery Challenge.

% =====================================================================
\section{Causal Graph Theory}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Directed Acyclic Graphs and Structural Causal Models}

Causal relationships between random variables are represented using a \textit{Directed Acyclic Graph} (DAG) $G=(V,E)$, where each edge $X_i \to X_j$ denotes a direct causal effect from $X_i$ to $X_j$. Combined with structural equations
\begin{equation}
  X_i := f_i\!\left(\mathrm{Pa}(X_i),\, U_i\right),
  \label{eq:scm}
\end{equation}
this forms a \textit{Structural Causal Model} (SCM)~\cite{pearl2009causality}, where $\mathrm{Pa}(X_i)$ is the set of direct parents of $X_i$ and $U_i$ is an independent exogenous noise term. SCMs clearly distinguish between the observational distribution $P(V)$ and the interventional distribution $P(V \mid do(X=x))$, which is the foundation for defining the causal meaning of each relationship in the graph~\cite{pearl2009causality}.

\subsection{d-Separation and the Three Fundamental Motifs}

Conditional independence on a DAG is derived via the \textit{d-separation} criterion~\cite{geiger2013dseparation}: a set $Z$ d-separates $X$ and $Y$ if every path between $X$ and $Y$ is blocked by $Z$. Under the Faithfulness assumption (every statistical independence reflects a corresponding graphical separation~\cite{spirtes2000causation}), one can reason from data back to graph structure.

Three fundamental motifs are directly relevant to classifying the role of variable $K$. \textbf{Chain:} $X \to K \to Y$. Conditioning on $K$ blocks the path: $X \perp Y \mid K$. This is the structure of the \textbf{Mediator} role. \textbf{Fork (Confounder):} $X \leftarrow K \to Y$. Conditioning on $K$ blocks the path: $X \perp Y \mid K$. This is the structure of the \textbf{Confounder} role. \textbf{Collider:} $X \to K \leftarrow Y$. Marginally: $X \perp Y$; but conditioning on $K$ induces dependence: $X \not\perp Y \mid K$ (collider bias). This is the structure of the \textbf{Collider} role.

Note that Chain and Fork produce the same marginal statistical signature ($X \perp Y \mid K$), while Collider produces the opposite. This leads to fundamental difficulty in distinguishing Mediator from Confounder using conditional independence tests alone.

\section{The Eight-Class Causal Role Classification Problem}
\label{sec:8roles}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Problem Setup from the ADIA Lab Causal Discovery Challenge}

The ADIA Lab Causal Discovery Challenge~\cite{crunchdao_adia_docs, crunchdao_competition} poses the following problem: given a dataset of $n = 1000$ observations over $p \in \{3, \ldots, 10\}$ variables, where two special variables $X$ (treatment) and $Y$ (outcome) are identified and the causal edge $X \to Y$ is assumed to exist, classify the role of each variable $K \neq X,\, K \neq Y$ with respect to the relationship $X \to Y$.

This is a \textbf{multiclass classification} problem: for each tuple $(X, Y, K, \mathcal{D})$, the model predicts one of 8 labels. There are 47,000 training datasets in total. The official evaluation metric is \textit{Multiclass Balanced Accuracy}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BA} = \frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=1}^{C} \frac{\mathrm{TP}_c}{\mathrm{TP}_c + \mathrm{FN}_c},
  \label{eq:balanced_acc}
\end{equation}
with $C = 8$ classes. This metric handles class imbalance naturally -- each class is weighted equally regardless of sample count~\cite{crunchdao_adia_docs, olivetti2025adialab}. A random guessing strategy achieves 12.5\% (1/8), and the organizer-provided baselines reach approximately 40\%~\cite{olivetti2025adialab}.

\subsection{Definition of the Eight Variable Roles}
\label{subsec:8classes}

The eight labels are defined according to the causal structure of $K$ in the DAG containing $X$, $Y$, and $K$ (with the edge $X \to Y$ always assumed present):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Confounder:} $K \to X$ and $K \to Y$. Variable $K$ is a common cause, inducing spurious correlation between $X$ and $Y$ if $K$ is not controlled for.
  \item \textbf{Mediator:} $X \to K \to Y$ (a mediation pathway parallel to the direct edge $X \to Y$). Variable $K$ transmits part of the causal effect from $X$ to $Y$.
  \item \textbf{Collider:} $X \to K \leftarrow Y$. Both $X$ and $Y$ are causes of $K$. Conditioning on $K$ induces spurious dependence between $X$ and $Y$.
  \item \textbf{Independent:} $K$ has no causal edges to or from $X$ or $Y$. $K$ is statistically independent of both.
  \item \textbf{Cause of X:} $K \to X$, but there is no direct path between $K$ and $Y$ other than through $X$.
  \item \textbf{Consequence of X:} $X \to K$, and $K$ has no effect on $Y$.
  \item \textbf{Cause of Y:} $K \to Y$, but there is no direct path between $K$ and $X$ other than through $Y$.
  \item \textbf{Consequence of Y:} $Y \to K$, and $K$ has no effect on $X$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Challenges in Distinguishing Between Classes}

Several pairs of labels share similar statistical signatures, making classification difficult. \textbf{Confounder vs.\ Cause of X:} Both have $K \to X$, creating correlation between $K$ and $X$. The difference lies in whether $K$ has an edge to $Y$. \textbf{Mediator vs.\ Consequence of X:} Both have $X \to K$. The difference lies in whether $K$ subsequently affects $Y$. \textbf{Mediator vs.\ Confounder:} As analyzed above, both produce $X \perp Y \mid K$ under d-separation, but through entirely different causal mechanisms.

According to the post-competition analysis by the organizers~\cite{olivetti2025adialab}, even the first-place solution achieved only 65.67\% on the Mediator class -- the lowest among all eight classes -- while achieving 86.18\% on Independent. The organizers' supervised baseline reached only 13.89\% on Mediator and 18.60\% on Confounder, confirming these as the two most challenging classes~\cite{olivetti2025adialab}. In the reimplementation results of this thesis, the two hardest classes are Mediator ($\sim\!56\%$) and Collider ($\sim\!63\%$).

\section{Edge Tensor Representation}
\label{sec:edge_tensor}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Motivation for the Edge-Based Approach}

Rather than processing a raw data matrix $(n \times p)$, the first-place solution~\cite{adia_rank1_report} represents each \textit{ordered variable pair} $(u, v)$ as a multi-channel \textbf{edge tensor} of length $n$. This approach has two advantages: (1) the architecture becomes \textit{permutation-equivariant} with respect to variable ordering; and (2) directional information for $(u \to v)$ and $(v \to u)$ is encoded separately, allowing the model to learn causal asymmetry.

In this thesis, the edge tensor is extended from 3 channels (original solution) to \textbf{8 channels}, adding multi-bandwidth kernel regression and multi-bandwidth ANM residual channels.

\subsection{The Eight Channels of the Edge Tensor}
\label{subsec:8channels}

Given variable pair $(u, v)$ and permutation $\sigma$ that sorts $u$ in ascending order, the 8-channel edge tensor is constructed as follows.

\paragraph{Channel 1 -- Sorted $u$.} The sequence $\mathbf{c}_1 = (u_{\sigma(1)}, \ldots, u_{\sigma(n)})$ preserves the marginal distribution of $u$.

\paragraph{Channel 2 -- $v$ sorted by rank of $u$.} The sequence $\mathbf{c}_2 = (v_{\sigma(1)}, \ldots, v_{\sigma(n)})$ encodes the monotonic relationship and rank correlation between $u$ and $v$.

\paragraph{Channels 3--5 -- Multi-bandwidth kernel regression.} At each point $u_{\sigma(i)}$, the Nadaraya--Watson estimator~\cite{nadaraya1964, watson1964}:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{m}_h(u_{\sigma(i)}) =
  \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(u_{\sigma(i)} - u_j)^2}{2h^2}\right) v_j}
       {\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(u_{\sigma(i)} - u_j)^2}{2h^2}\right)},
  \label{eq:nadaraya}
\end{equation}
is computed with three bandwidths $h \in \{0.2,\, 0.5,\, 1.0\}$, producing channels $\mathbf{c}_3, \mathbf{c}_4, \mathbf{c}_5$. Each bandwidth captures a different smoothness scale of the regression function -- $h = 0.2$ is sensitive to local structure while $h = 1.0$ reflects global trends. Using multiple bandwidths prevents the model from depending on any single fixed choice of $h$.

\paragraph{Channels 6--8 -- Multi-bandwidth ANM residuals.} The Additive Noise Model (ANM)~\cite{shimizu2006lingam} assumes $V = f(U) + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \perp U$. The regression residual in direction $u \to v$ at point $u_{\sigma(i)}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  r_h(u_{\sigma(i)}) = v_{\sigma(i)} - \hat{m}_h(u_{\sigma(i)}),
  \label{eq:anm_resid}
\end{equation}
computed with the same three bandwidths, producing channels $\mathbf{c}_6, \mathbf{c}_7, \mathbf{c}_8$.

The causal asymmetry of ANM residuals is what makes this channel informative: in a model $U \to V$ with non-Gaussian noise, the residual $V - \hat{m}(U)$ is closer to independent of $U$ than the reverse direction $U - \hat{m}(V)$~\cite{shimizu2006lingam}. The Conv1D model can learn this signature from the distributional shape of $\mathbf{c}_{6..8}$. Experiments show that ANM channels improve Collider accuracy by $+6.1\%$ and Consequence of X by $+5.0\%$ compared to the 3-channel baseline.

\section{Core Deep Learning Architectural Components}
\label{sec:deep_arch}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{1D Convolutional Networks and Residual Blocks}

1D convolutional networks apply learned filters over 1D sequences, making them well-suited to extract local features from edge tensors (each edge is a sequence of length $n = 1000$). With a filter of width $k$, a Conv1D layer computes:
\begin{equation}
  y_t = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} w_j \cdot x_{t+j} + b,
  \label{eq:conv1d}
\end{equation}
yielding a feature representation at each position in the input sequence.

To avoid vanishing gradients as network depth increases, \textit{skip connections} (residual connections)~\cite{he2016resnet} are used:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y} = \mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x};\, \{W_i\}) + \mathbf{x},
  \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}$ is the learned mapping and $\mathbf{x}$ is the shortcut input. Combined with \textit{Group Normalization}~\cite{wu2018groupnorm} (replacing Batch Normalization for stability with small batch sizes) and the GELU activation function~\cite{hendrycks2016gelu}, each residual block in the model takes the form:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x} + \mathrm{Conv1D}\!\left(\mathrm{GELU}\!\left(\mathrm{GroupNorm}(\mathbf{x})\right)\right).
  \label{eq:residual_block}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Self-Attention Mechanism}
\label{subsec:selfattention}

After each edge tensor is processed through Conv1D blocks and reduced to a representation vector $\mathbf{e}_{uv} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ via Global Average Pooling, information from all edges in the graph must be aggregated to reason about each variable's role. The \textit{self-attention} mechanism~\cite{vaswani2017attention} allows each element in a sequence to query information from all other elements:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V,
  \label{eq:attention}
\end{equation}
where $Q = \mathbf{X} W^Q$, $K = \mathbf{X} W^K$, $V = \mathbf{X} W^V$ are linear projections.

\textit{Multi-head attention}~\cite{vaswani2017attention} runs $H$ attention heads in parallel:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MultiHead}(Q,K,V) = \mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_H)\,W^O.
  \label{eq:multihead}
\end{equation}
Two layers of multi-head self-attention are placed after the pooling step to aggregate edge-level representations across all edges in a dataset, enabling the model to compare graph-wide information and transition from edge-level to node-level representations.

\subsection{Loss Function and Training Strategy}
\label{subsec:training}

The model uses two output heads with two loss functions. The \textbf{Node head} (8-class) is the primary loss: Cross-Entropy (CE) with inverse-frequency class weights. The \textbf{Edge head} (binary -- causal edge present or not) is an auxiliary loss used only during training. The combined loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{node}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{edge}},
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a balancing hyperparameter. Class weights in CE are set inversely proportional to class frequency in the training set to handle class imbalance. Optimization uses \textit{AdamW}~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw} with a \textit{cosine annealing} schedule~\cite{loshchilov2017sgdr}.

\section{Existing Approaches on the ADIA Lab Challenge}
\label{sec:related_work}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{First-Place Solution: End-to-End Deep Learning}
\label{subsec:rank1}

The first-place solution~\cite{adia_rank1_report} is an end-to-end deep learning model with the following architecture: \textit{Edge tensor construction} (3 channels) $\to$ \textit{Stem Conv1D} (3$\to$64 channels) $\to$ 5 \textit{Residual Conv1D blocks} $\to$ \textit{Global Average Pooling} $\to$ \textit{Merge with edge-type embedding} $\to$ 2 \textit{Self-Attention layers} $\to$ \textit{Node head} (8-class) + \textit{Edge head} (binary, auxiliary). The model has approximately 188K parameters and achieves \textbf{76.7\%} Balanced Accuracy on the public leaderboard.

\subsection{Rank 3--5 Solutions: Feature Engineering with Gradient Boosting}

The rank-4~\cite{adia_rank4_medium} and rank-5~\cite{adia_rank5_medium} solutions follow a feature engineering + gradient boosting approach: extracting statistical features (Pearson/Spearman correlation, partial correlation, conditional mutual information, HSIC, regression residuals) together with signals from classical causal discovery algorithms (LiNGAM~\cite{shimizu2006lingam}, GES), then using LightGBM/XGBoost ensembles for 8-class classification. Advantages include interpretability and no GPU requirement; disadvantages include dependence on manual feature engineering and the inability to learn representations end-to-end.

\subsection{Position of This Thesis}

This thesis reimplements and analyzes the first-place solution in detail, then proposes improvements to raise Balanced Accuracy -- particularly on the difficult classes Mediator ($\sim\!56\%$) and Collider ($\sim\!63\%$) in the reimplementation baseline. The baseline score achieved in this thesis is \textbf{73.96\%} local / \textbf{73.61\%} public leaderboard. The specific technical contributions are presented in Chapter~\ref{chap:method}.
