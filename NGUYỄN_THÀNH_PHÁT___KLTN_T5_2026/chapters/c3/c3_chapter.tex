\chapter{Proposed Methods for Causal Role Classification}
\label{chap:method}

This chapter describes the complete pipeline of the model developed in this thesis, from data preprocessing, feature construction, and neural network architecture, to training strategy and proposed improvements. The thesis reimplements the first-place solution~\cite{adia_rank1_report, olivetti2025adialab} as a baseline, then proposes two primary improvements: (1) extending the edge tensor from 3 to \textbf{8 channels} by adding multi-bandwidth kernel regression and multi-bandwidth ANM residual channels; and (2) data augmentation via \textbf{X/Y remap augmentation} ($\sim\!11.2\times$ training samples). A structural attention bias mechanism is additionally proposed as a topology-aware architectural improvement.

% =====================================================================
\section{Architecture Overview}
\label{sec:overview}
% =====================================================================

Given an input dataset of $n = 1000$ observations over $p$ variables (with $X$ and $Y$ pre-identified), the pipeline consists of four main stages.

The first stage is \textbf{edge tensor construction}: for each ordered variable pair $(u, v)$ in the complete directed graph over $p$ nodes, an 8-channel tensor of length $n$ is constructed encoding the statistical relationship between $u$ and $v$. The second stage is \textbf{per-edge feature extraction}: each edge tensor is processed independently through a Stem Conv1D and 5 residual Conv1D blocks, then pooled to a $d$-dimensional vector via Global Average Pooling. The third stage is \textbf{graph-wide information aggregation}: edge representation vectors are combined with edge-type embeddings and passed through 2 Multi-head Self-Attention layers, allowing the model to compare information across all variable pairs and learn node-level representations from edge-level information. The fourth stage is \textbf{classification} through two parallel output heads -- the \textit{node head} (8 classes, primary output) and the \textit{edge head} (binary, training only).

The model has approximately $\sim\!188$K parameters, uses no pretrained weights, and is trained entirely from scratch on the ADIA Lab dataset.

\section{Preprocessing and Edge Tensor Construction}
\label{sec:preprocessing}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Data Normalization}

Each input dataset is a matrix $\mathbf{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ with $n = 1000$ observations and $p \in \{3,\ldots,10\}$ variables. Before edge tensor construction, each column (variable) is standardized to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ via z-score normalization:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma + \varepsilon},
\end{equation}
where $\mu$, $\sigma$ are the column mean and standard deviation, and $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$ prevents division by zero. Normalization stabilizes the model and reduces sensitivity to the original variable scales.

\subsection{Complete Directed Graph and Edge Pairs}

From $p$ variables, a complete directed graph with $p(p-1)$ edges is constructed (each pair $(u, v)$ with $u \neq v$ contributes two edges in both directions). Considering all ordered pairs -- rather than unordered pairs -- is necessary because the kernel regression channel is directionally asymmetric: $\hat{m}_{u \to v}(u_i) \neq \hat{m}_{v \to u}(v_i)$ in general, and this asymmetry carries the signal about causal direction.

\subsection{Channels 1--2: Sorted Variables}

Let $\sigma$ be the permutation such that $u_{\sigma(1)} \leq \cdots \leq u_{\sigma(n)}$. The first two channels are:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{c}_1 = (u_{\sigma(1)},\ldots,u_{\sigma(n)}), \qquad
    \mathbf{c}_2 = (v_{\sigma(1)},\ldots,v_{\sigma(n)}).
\end{equation}
$\mathbf{c}_1$ preserves the marginal distribution of $u$; $\mathbf{c}_2$ encodes the monotonic relationship and rank correlation between $u$ and $v$, serving as an empirical copula representation of the pair $(u,v)$.

\subsection{Channels 3--5: Multi-Bandwidth Kernel Regression}
\label{subsec:channel_kernel}

At each point $u_{\sigma(i)}$, the Nadaraya--Watson estimate~\cite{nadaraya1964, watson1964}:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{m}_h(u_{\sigma(i)}) =
    \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(u_{\sigma(i)}-u_j)^2}{2h^2}\right) v_j}
         {\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{n} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(u_{\sigma(i)}-u_j)^2}{2h^2}\right)},
    \label{eq:nw_ch}
\end{equation}
is computed with three bandwidths $h \in \{0.2,\, 0.5,\, 1.0\}$, yielding three channels:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{c}_{2+k} = \bigl(\hat{m}_{h_k}(u_{\sigma(1)}),\ldots,\hat{m}_{h_k}(u_{\sigma(n)})\bigr),
    \quad k=1,2,3.
\end{equation}
Each bandwidth captures a different smoothness level: $h=0.2$ is sensitive to local structure, $h=1.0$ reflects global trends. Using multiple bandwidths prevents dependence on any single fixed $h$ and broadens the representational coverage of the features.

\subsection{Channels 6--8: Multi-Bandwidth ANM Residuals}
\label{subsec:channel_anm}

The regression residual in direction $u \to v$ at point $u_{\sigma(i)}$:
\begin{equation}
    r_h(u_{\sigma(i)}) = v_{\sigma(i)} - \hat{m}_h(u_{\sigma(i)}),
    \label{eq:anm_resid}
\end{equation}
produces three channels $\mathbf{c}_6, \mathbf{c}_7, \mathbf{c}_8$ with $h \in \{0.2,\, 0.5,\, 1.0\}$.

According to the ANM framework~\cite{shimizu2006lingam}, if $U \to V$ is the true causal direction, then the residual $V - \hat{m}(U)$ is closer to being independent of $U$ than the reverse direction residual $U - \hat{m}(V)$, which will exhibit stronger nonlinear dependence. This is a powerful asymmetric signal for distinguishing edge direction. Experiments show that adding 3 ANM channels improves Collider accuracy by $+6.1\%$ and Consequence of X by $+5.0\%$ compared to using only the 3 original channels.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/edge_tensor_channels.png}
    \caption{Illustration of 8-channel edge tensor construction for variable pair $(u, v)$: channels 1--2 are rank-sorted sequences, channels 3--5 are Nadaraya--Watson kernel regression with bandwidths $h \in \{0.2, 0.5, 1.0\}$, channels 6--8 are ANM residuals with the same three bandwidths.}
    \label{fig:edge_tensor}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Parallel Implementation}

With 23,500 training datasets and $p(p-1)$ edges per dataset, constructing edge tensors (particularly channels 3--8 involving $n=1000$ points) requires substantial computation if done sequentially. The thesis uses \texttt{ProcessPoolExecutor} with the \texttt{fork} start method to parallelize preprocessing across multiple CPU cores. In the DataLoader, \texttt{num\_workers=0} is used to avoid shared memory conflicts with the preprocessing multiprocessing step. % A critical implementation detail: \texttt{OPENBLAS\_NUM\_THREADS=1} must be set before importing NumPy to prevent fork deadlocks caused by OpenBLAS's internal thread pools.

\section{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Stem Layer}

The network input is an edge tensor $\mathbf{T} \in \mathbb{R}^{8 \times n}$ (8 channels, sequence length $n = 1000$). The stem layer is a single Conv1D that expands from 8 input channels to 64 hidden channels:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{H}_0 = \mathrm{Conv1D}_{8 \to 64}(\mathbf{T}) \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times n}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Five Residual Conv1D Blocks}

Five residual blocks process $\mathbf{H}_0$ and extract increasingly abstract local features with depth. Each block has the structure:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{H}_{l+1} = \mathbf{H}_l +
    \mathrm{Conv1D}\!\left(\mathrm{GELU}\!\left(\mathrm{GroupNorm}(\mathbf{H}_l)\right)\right),
    \quad l = 0,1,\ldots,4,
\end{equation}
with skip connections ensuring stable gradient flow~\cite{he2016resnet}. Group Normalization~\cite{wu2018groupnorm} is used instead of Batch Normalization because it is more stable with small batch sizes ($\mathrm{bs} = 16$\,--\,64) -- Batch Norm requires large batches to estimate running statistics accurately. The GELU activation~\cite{hendrycks2016gelu} provides smoother gradients than ReLU near zero, which is appropriate for sequences with negative values (after z-score normalization). After 5 blocks: $\mathbf{H}_5 \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times n}$.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/residual_block.png}
    \caption{Structure of a single residual Conv1D block: GroupNorm $\to$ GELU $\to$ Conv1D with a direct additive skip connection.}
    \label{fig:residual_block}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Global Average Pooling and the Merge Operator}
\label{subsec:merge}

Global Average Pooling reduces the sequence dimension to a fixed-size vector:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{e}_{uv} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathbf{H}_5[\,:,\,t] \in \mathbb{R}^{64}.
\end{equation}

At this point, $\mathbf{e}_{uv}$ encodes only the \textit{content} of the statistical relationship between $u$ and $v$, but not the \textit{semantic position} of the edge in the graph -- that is, which types of nodes it connects. For example, the edge $X \to K$ (treatment to intermediate variable) carries a very different meaning from $K \to Y$ (intermediate to outcome) or $K_i \to K_j$ (between two non-special variables), even if the three edges have similar statistical distributions.

To encode this information, each edge $(u, v)$ is assigned an \textbf{edge type} $\tau_{uv} \in \{0, 1, \ldots, T-1\}$ based on the identity of the two endpoint nodes ($X$, $Y$, or a variable $K$). A learnable lookup table maps each edge type to an embedding vector $\mathbf{t}_{\tau_{uv}} \in \mathbb{R}^{64}$.

The \textbf{Merge operator} adds the two vectors:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{e}}_{uv} = \mathbf{e}_{uv} + \mathbf{t}_{\tau_{uv}},
    \label{eq:merge}
\end{equation}
producing an edge representation $\tilde{\mathbf{e}}_{uv} \in \mathbb{R}^{64}$ that integrates both statistical information (from Conv1D) and semantic positional information (from edge-type embedding). Addition rather than concatenation preserves the representation dimension and allows the model to learn flexible combinations of the two information sources. The set $\{\tilde{\mathbf{e}}_{uv}\}_{u \neq v}$ is then passed to the self-attention layers.

\subsection{Two Multi-Head Self-Attention Layers}

The set of all edge representations in a dataset forms a sequence $\mathbf{E} = \{\tilde{\mathbf{e}}_{uv}\}_{u \neq v} \in \mathbb{R}^{p(p-1) \times d}$. Two Multi-head Self-Attention layers~\cite{vaswani2017attention} allow each edge to exchange information with all other edges in the dataset, aggregating global information about the graph structure:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{E}' = \mathrm{MultiHeadSelfAttention}(\mathbf{E}).
\end{equation}
This component is critical for achieving \textit{permutation equivariance}: the classification result for node $K$ does not depend on the naming order of variables in the dataset, which is essential given that $p$ ranges from 3 to 10.

\subsection{Dual-Head Output}

From the representation obtained after self-attention, the model has two parallel output heads. The \textit{node head} is a Linear layer projecting to 8 logits corresponding to the 8 causal roles; this is the primary output, used during both training and inference. The \textit{edge head} is a Linear layer projecting to 2 logits (causal edge present or absent); this is an auxiliary head that contributes only to the training loss and is discarded during inference. The auxiliary head provides an additional learning signal about edge structure, helping to stabilize training particularly in early epochs when the model has not yet learned good representations for 8-class classification.

\section{Loss Function and Class Imbalance Handling}
\label{sec:loss}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Inverse-Frequency Weighted Cross-Entropy}

The eight classes in this problem are not uniformly distributed -- classes such as Mediator and Collider have significantly fewer samples than Independent or Confounder. Standard Cross-Entropy would cause the model to favor majority classes, resulting in low Balanced Accuracy despite potentially high overall accuracy.

To address this, each class $c$ is assigned a weight inversely proportional to its frequency:
\begin{equation}
    w_c = \frac{1}{f_c \cdot C},
    \label{eq:class_weight}
\end{equation}
where $f_c$ is the fraction of class $c$ samples in the training set and $C = 8$. The node head loss becomes:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{node}} = -\sum_{c=1}^{C} w_c \cdot \mathbb{1}[y = c]\, \log \hat{p}_c.
    \label{eq:weighted_ce}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Combined Loss}

The final loss combines both output heads:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{node}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{edge}},
    \label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{edge}}$ is Binary Cross-Entropy with similar inverse-frequency weighting, and $\lambda$ is the balancing hyperparameter.

\section{Training Strategy}
\label{sec:training_strategy}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Hyperparameter Configuration}

The configuration achieving the best results on the v11 + XY augmentation model is: batch size $= 64$, learning rate $\alpha_{\max} = 10^{-3}$, number of epochs $= 10$, training on the augmented set of $\sim\!263$K samples (6 shards $\times$ 50K). For the base model (without augmentation), the configuration is: batch size $= 16$, learning rate $\alpha_{\max} = 10^{-3}$, number of epochs $= 20$--$30$, training on the full 23,500 samples.

\subsection{AdamW and Cosine Annealing}

The model is optimized with \textit{AdamW}~\cite{loshchilov2019adamw}, a variant of Adam that decouples weight decay from gradient updates, enabling more effective regularization and preventing overfitting on large datasets. The learning rate follows a \textit{cosine annealing schedule}~\cite{loshchilov2017sgdr}:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_t = \alpha_{\min} + \frac{1}{2}(\alpha_{\max} - \alpha_{\min})
    \left(1 + \cos\!\left(\frac{t}{T_{\max}}\pi\right)\right),
    \label{eq:cosine_lr}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\max}$ is the total number of training steps. Cosine annealing decreases the learning rate smoothly from $\alpha_{\max}$ to $\alpha_{\min}$, enabling stable convergence at the end of training without getting stuck in poor local minima as with step decay schedules.

\subsection{Distributed Training for Large-Scale Augmentation}

Training on the $\sim\!263$K augmented dataset (stored as 6 shards of $\sim\!50$K samples each) requires Distributed Data Parallel (DDP) training across two GPUs, as the dataset size exceeds single-GPU memory. A custom \texttt{ShardGroupedSampler} ensures that samples from the same shard are batched together to minimize disk I/O. Important implementation notes for DDP: \texttt{use\_distributed\_sampler=False} when using the custom sampler, strip \texttt{module.} prefix when saving model weights, and the \texttt{LOCAL\_RANK} environment variable guards the evaluation step so only rank-0 performs evaluation.

\section{X/Y Remap Data Augmentation}
\label{sec:xyaug}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

With 23,500 training datasets, each dataset has only one pre-defined pair $(X, Y)$. However, in the ground-truth DAG, any directed edge $A \to B$ can serve as a valid treatment-outcome pair if we relabel $X' = A$, $Y' = B$. This allows generating many new classification problems from the same DAG without collecting additional observational data.

\subsection{Augmentation Procedure}

For each original dataset with DAG $G$ and pair $(X, Y)$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Enumerate all directed edges $A \to B$ in $G$ (excluding $X \to Y$).
    \item For each pair $(A, B)$: set $X' = A$, $Y' = B$, and re-derive 8-class labels for all variables $K \neq A, K \neq B$ according to the rule table in ~\ref{subsec:label_derivation}.
    \item Keep the observation matrix $\mathbf{D}$ unchanged -- only the semantic roles of $X'$ and $Y'$ change.
\end{enumerate}

The number of augmented datasets grows by approximately $\sim\!11.2\times$ on average compared to the original set (as each DAG has approximately 10 edges besides $X \to Y$). From 23,500 original datasets, approximately $\sim\!263$K training samples are obtained.

\subsection{Efficient Implementation}

The key efficiency insight is that the kernel regression (channels 3--8) is computed \textit{once} over the full observation matrix $\mathbf{D}$ before generating augmented samples. For each new pair $(X', Y')$, only label re-derivation from the adjacency matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is needed -- no recomputation of kernels. This makes augmentation computationally near-free once the base edge tensors are built.

The augmented set is stored as \textit{shards}: 6 files each containing $\sim\!50$K samples, avoiding OOM when loading into RAM. During training, \texttt{ShardGroupedSampler} ensures that samples from the same shard are batched together to optimize disk loading.

\section{Structural Attention Bias}
\label{sec:structbias}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

Standard multi-head self-attention treats all edge pairs equally when computing attention weights, regardless of their topological relationship in the graph. However, edges that share a common endpoint -- or form a chain through $X \to K \to Y$ -- are causal structurally more relevant to each other than unrelated edges. This prior knowledge about causal topology can be injected into the attention mechanism at minimal parameter cost.

\subsection{Implementation}

For each pair of edges $(u_1, v_1)$ and $(u_2, v_2)$, six topological relationship types $\tau$ are defined based on shared endpoints: \textit{reverse} (same pair, opposite direction), \textit{shared-source} ($u_1 = u_2$), \textit{shared-target} ($v_1 = v_2$), \textit{forward-chain} ($v_1 = u_2$), \textit{backward-chain} ($v_2 = u_1$), and \textit{unrelated}. The attention logit is adjusted by a learned scalar bias:
\begin{equation}
  A_{ij} = \frac{q_i \cdot k_j + b_{\tau(i,j)}}{\sqrt{d}},
  \label{eq:v11_bias}
\end{equation}
where $b_\tau \in \mathbb{R}$ is a learned bias for each type $\tau$. The bias matrix is built in the \texttt{collate\_fn} (not stored per sample) and is cached per graph size $p$ since there are only 3--10 possible values of $p$. Bias weights are initialized to zero so training starts from standard attention.

This differs fundamentally from the failed architectural experiments (v3+, v4): rather than allowing attention to learn everything from raw observation data, structural bias only learns the \textit{degree of priority among topological types}, keeping the parameter space small with explicit causal prior.

\section{Additional Experiments and Negative Results}
\label{sec:improvements}
% =====================================================================

Beyond the primary improvements described above, the thesis investigates several other architectural directions. Their results are predominantly negative, constituting important findings about the nature of this problem.

\subsection{Observation-Level Cross-Attention}

One direction explored is adding a branch that performs cross-attention over the raw $n=1000$ observations, rather than just pooling them. The hypothesis is that attention over observation pairs could learn conditional independence patterns without explicit feature engineering. However, this approach (v3+, v4) consistently underperforms: without strong inductive bias about which observation pairs matter causally, the attention learns noise and overfits, as evidenced by large gaps between local and public scores.

\subsection{Full ML Pipeline with Gradient Boosting}

The thesis also tests a purely tabular machine learning approach (v13): a 300+ feature pipeline combining correlation statistics, partial correlation, CMI, ANM scores, BIC scores, and outputs from classical causal discovery algorithms (PC, LiNGAM, NOTEARS, GES), fed into an XGBoost/LightGBM/CatBoost ensemble with an additional GNN refinement stage. Despite substantial engineering effort, this achieves only 72.64\% -- confirming that the Conv1D architecture's ability to learn from nonlinear functional curves cannot be replicated by scalar feature engineering alone.

\subsection{GNN Refinement on Predicted Graph}

After the node head produces 8-class logits for each variable $K$, current predictions are made independently per variable without checking structural consistency across variables. A lightweight Graph Neural Network (GNN) refinement pass could be added to adjust predictions based on graph-level constraints: for example, if $K_1$ is predicted as a Collider of $X \to Y$, it must receive edges from both $X$ and $Y$, affecting the edge head predictions of related edges. This remains a promising direction that was not fully developed within the scope of this thesis.
