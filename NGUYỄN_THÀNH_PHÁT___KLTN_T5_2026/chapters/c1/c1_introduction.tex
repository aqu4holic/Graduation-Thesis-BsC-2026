\section{Problem Statement}
% =====================================================================

In data science and statistics, understanding \textit{causal relationships} between variables is far more important than merely measuring correlation. Correlation can arise from many sources -- hidden common causes (confounders), mediation, or pure statistical coincidence -- whereas causal relationships reflect the true underlying mechanism of a system~\cite{pearl2009causality}. Correctly identifying causal structure is foundational to many practical problems: experimental design, policy impact evaluation, financial risk analysis, and causal inference in medicine~\cite{hasan2023survey_arxiv}.

The problem studied in this thesis is grounded in the \textbf{ADIA Lab Causal Discovery Challenge}~\cite{crunchdao_causal_2024, crunchdao_adia_docs}, a data science competition organized by ADIA Lab in collaboration with CrunchDAO. Specifically, the task is: given a dataset of $n = 1000$ observations over $p \in \{3,\ldots,10\}$ variables, where a causal edge $X \to Y$ always exists (with $X$ the treatment variable and $Y$ the outcome), \textbf{classify the role} of each remaining variable $K$ into one of \textbf{8 classes}: Confounder, Mediator, Collider, Independent, Cause of X, Consequence of X, Cause of Y, or Consequence of Y.

This is a \textit{supervised multiclass classification} problem: given 47,000 training datasets, the model must learn statistical features and latent graph structure to distinguish among eight roles -- several of which share very similar statistical signatures and are inherently difficult to separate. Performance is measured by \textit{Multiclass Balanced Accuracy}~\cite{crunchdao_adia_docs}.

\section{Motivation and Challenges}
% =====================================================================

Although many classical causal inference algorithms have been proposed (PC, LiNGAM, NOTEARS, etc.), these methods typically aim to recover the \textit{full DAG structure} and struggle when the goal is to precisely classify \textit{the role of each individual variable} from finite-sample observational data~\cite{spirtes2000causation}.

The ADIA Lab problem poses several specific challenges. First, \textbf{distinguishing between similar class pairs}: for example, Confounder ($K \to X$, $K \to Y$) and Cause of X ($K \to X$, no $K \to Y$) both induce correlation between $K$ and $X$; similarly, Mediator ($X \to K \to Y$) and Consequence of X ($X \to K$, no effect on $Y$) both exhibit $X \to K$ -- the distinction lies in very subtle signals within the data. Second, \textbf{class imbalance}: the eight classes are not uniformly distributed, with Mediator and Collider having significantly fewer samples than others, causing models to neglect minority classes without appropriate handling strategies. Third, \textbf{permutation invariance}: the number of variables $p$ varies from 3 to 10, and variable naming order carries no semantic meaning -- the model must be invariant to variable permutation. Fourth, \textbf{nonlinear relationships}: the dependencies between variables are not necessarily linear, and simple statistics such as Pearson correlation are often insufficient to distinguish roles.

Classical solutions provided by the competition organizers (PC algorithm, Random Forest, deep learning baseline) achieved only around \textbf{40\%} Balanced Accuracy~\cite{olivetti2025adialab} -- not far above random guessing at 12.5\% (1/8). The competition's first-place solution~\cite{adia_rank1_report} addressed these challenges through an end-to-end deep learning architecture: representing each variable pair as a \textit{3-channel edge tensor} (incorporating nonlinear kernel regression features), then using residual Conv1D and self-attention to learn graph-wide representations and classify each variable's role, achieving \textbf{76.70\%} Balanced Accuracy~\cite{olivetti2025adialab}. This thesis reimplements that solution, analyzes its components, and proposes extensions to an 8-channel feature representation combined with X/Y remap data augmentation, ultimately achieving \textbf{80.82\%} on the public leaderboard -- surpassing the first-place solution.

\section{Objectives and Scope}
% =====================================================================

This thesis has two primary objectives. First, to \textbf{reimplement and analyze} the first-place solution of the ADIA Lab Causal Discovery Challenge: understanding each architectural component, the training procedure, and the rationale behind each design choice. This objective produces a solid, reproducible baseline. Second, to \textbf{propose improvements} aimed at increasing Balanced Accuracy, particularly on difficult classes (Mediator, Collider), through additional feature channels, architectural refinements, and training strategies.

\textbf{Scope:} The thesis focuses on i.i.d.\ observational data following the ADIA Lab setup (not time-series with autocorrelation). The model is implemented and evaluated on the CrunchDAO platform using the official Multiclass Balanced Accuracy metric.

\section{Contributions}
% =====================================================================

The specific contributions of this thesis are as follows.

\textbf{Reimplementation and detailed analysis of the first-place solution:} The complete pipeline is reimplemented -- from edge tensor construction (3 channels, kernel regression) to the residual Conv1D + self-attention + dual-head loss architecture -- achieving \textbf{73.96\%} (local) / \textbf{73.61\%} (public leaderboard), confirming the reproducibility of the original solution.

\textbf{Extension of edge tensors to 8 channels:} Three multi-bandwidth kernel regression channels ($h \in \{0.2, 0.5, 1.0\}$) and three multi-bandwidth ANM (Additive Noise Model) residual channels are added, enhancing the model's ability to detect causal asymmetry and distinguish difficult classes such as Collider and Consequence of X.

\textbf{X/Y remap data augmentation strategy:} A data augmentation technique is proposed and implemented by treating each directed edge $A \to B$ in the ground-truth DAG as a new treatment-outcome pair $(X', Y')$, recomputing 8-class labels for all remaining variables relative to the new pair, yielding approximately $11.2\times$ additional training samples.

\textbf{Structural attention bias:} A topology-aware attention bias is introduced into the self-attention layers, encoding prior knowledge about causal relationships between edge pairs (chain, fork, collider) directly into the architecture with minimal additional parameters.

\textbf{Results surpassing the first-place solution:} The best proposed model (v11 + XY augmentation) achieves \textbf{81.14\%} (local) / \textbf{80.82\%} (public leaderboard), surpassing the competition's first-place solution (\textbf{76.70\%}) by a margin of $+4.12\%$~\cite{olivetti2025adialab}.
